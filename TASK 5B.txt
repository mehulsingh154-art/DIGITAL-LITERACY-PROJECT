Phishing is a cybercrime in which attackers impersonate a trusted person or organization to trick victims into revealing sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, or bank details, or into transferring money. The attack usually begins with a deceptive email, text message, or social‑media message that appears to come from a legitimate source like a bank, university, or online service.

A typical phishing attack unfolds in steps. First, the attacker gathers basic information about the target, such as their email address or workplace, often from public sources or data leaks. Next, they craft a message that looks authentic, using correct logos, similar domain names, and urgent language (for example, “Your account will be locked unless you act now”). The message includes a link to a fake website or a malicious attachment. When the victim clicks the link, they are directed to a counterfeit login page that captures their credentials, or the attachment installs malware that steals data. Finally, the attacker uses the stolen information to access accounts, commit fraud, or launch further attacks inside an organization.

College students, office workers, and online shoppers are common targets because they frequently share personal and financial data online and may not yet recognize subtle signs of fraud. Victims can suffer consequences such as drained bank accounts, identity theft, damaged credit scores, and loss of private files; in institutional cases, phishing can also lead to data breaches affecting thousands of users and costly security overhauls.
